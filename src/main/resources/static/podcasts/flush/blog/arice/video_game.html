Why I Lost Interest in Video Games

by A. Rice

06/30/2020

I have been playing video games since I was roughly 8 years of age. From then to the age of 18, I casually played console games. Then, I built my first PC and got into more time-consuming gaming. These games included Rainbow Six Siege, World of Warcraft, Call of Duty, Battlefront, and Battlefield. As for single-players,I enjoyed playing strategy games such as XCOM, Civilization, and Total War. I put in thousands of hours and hundreds of dollars into my Steam, Origin, and other platforms in the process. This was the norm for me until my senior year of college.

At this stage in my life, the ever-looming adult world was near, and the life of a student—a lifestyle that has been my life for nearly my entire existence—was ending. Finishing the year strong, finding a career, and staying fit for the Army National Guard were consistently on my mind. Because of this, my time gaming drastically decreased as my priorities shifted to put more time into tasks to further these goals.

Fast forward a few months, and the dust has settled—I graduated, found a satisfying career, and passed my yearly physical training test. Therefore, gaming fully returned to its place of power in my life again, right? Nope. The competitive shooter games were the first casualties. Simply put, I found the repetitive nature of FPS games boring. You spawn in, kill some stuff, get killed, and repeat. The occasional kill streak or close game brought some excitement, but it offered no new challenges, stimulation, or anything memorable. Perhaps another reason is because I reached what is the pinnacle of my skill in R6 back in college and made platinum rank. Not the most impressive thing in the world, but a huge personal achievement (which took several months to achieve) for me nonetheless. After that, though, what is next? I no longer had the time to grasp for a higher rank, and neither did my teammates.

My experience with strategy/single player games did not meet the same fate. I found the design of these games to consistently challenge me in thoughtful ways with their long, drawn out campaign games having a lot of replay ability due to different difficulties, factions, and play styles. Being able to save and pick up the game again as I pleased allowed these games to fit into my busier schedule. The need to know the latest patch notes or to understand the constantly shifting meta of online games was an issue, though. Even though single-player games filled my personal needs better, I was still playing them much less than before.

I believe this to have been a realization of how my time used for hobbies could turn into long lasting products or investments. For example, I play airsoft and make videos about it. This not only increases my fitness, but also my video editing skills. Also, the YouTube channel’s growth from this is far more rewarding than any long session of playing Call of Duty and unlocking a weapon skin. Painting my Warhammer models also brings a level of gratification, allowing me to see my painting abilities increase. Seeing these models every time I play a game with them is a constant reminder of the fruits of my labor. Participating in a podcast or a blog does something similar; it adds to a meaningful skill and provides long term gratification.

That is the key to me: long-term vs. short-term gratification and personal development. Do not get me wrong—video games, especially strategy games, have taught me a lot about critical thinking and following directions, but they ran their course for me. A good match or a good game only grants satisfaction while you’re doing good during and shortly after the match. The competitive skills I obtain from playing the game will always be numbered by high school kids or anyone else who has more time to invest into games than I do. The only real long-term gratification comes from being a streamer or going pro. In the instance of MMOs, my entire grind means nothing when new expansion releases and free characters are handed out, undermining and devaluing all my hard work.

I am not trying to sound high and mighty, right, or as though my way of thinking is the only correct one. Nor am I trying to say strategy games are better than others or that games you play are pointless. I am simply trying to do a deep dive into my own reasons that caused something that was so huge in my life to slip away. What matters is you are living your life the way you want to live it. Personally, as my life changed, so did my interest.
